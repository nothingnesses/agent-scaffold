# Q-42 exploration: a checkable receipt that the human-input contract was presented (skeptical / minimalist lens)

Explorer lens: skeptical / minimalist, optimising for Project Principle 2 (minimal by default; a feature must earn its place and must not complicate the core) and Principle 16 (one source of truth). This document stress-tests whether a decision receipt is worth building at all, and argues the null / light end of the space is the right home unless a concrete gap forces more.

## 1. The question, restated, and what is fixed

The human observed (2026-07-18) that the `Q-40` decision was sound BECAUSE they were presented the viable options, each option's trade-offs, a recommendation, and principle-judged reasoning before deciding, and asked whether the workflow should GUARANTEE that presentation every time a human decides, backed by a durable, checkable RECEIPT that it happened. This is the "rule, then make it checkable" pattern the project already applied to convergence: the human-input contract is the rule (`AGENTS.md:41`, "the viable options or approaches, the trade-offs of each, a recommendation, and the reasoning ... judged against the plan's numbered Project Principles"), and `validate --workflow` / W3 (`src/workflow.rs:135-212`) is the checkable form of the convergence rule.

Fixed, not to be reopened (from the `Q-42` item, `docs/plans/agent-scaffold.md:121`):

- A receipt does NOT live in the ledger. The ledger is per-task and deleted at task close, while decisions are permanent, and most decisions happen outside any review loop where no ledger exists. The ledger at most narrates a mid-loop decision (a cap escalation, an accept-risk) as a POINTER to the durable receipt/queue. So the design space is confined to the PERMANENT homes: the Open Questions queue and/or the permanent metrics JSONL round log.
- The NULL option (existing machinery already suffices; build nothing, or only a light check) is a first-class candidate, not a strawman.

What the human wants answered head-on (their four uncertainties): the PURPOSE and what it affords; the TRADE-OFFS (Principle 2 ceremony vs benefit); the concrete FORM; and the RELATION to the plan, especially the Open Questions section and Principle 16 (is a receipt a duplicate of the decided queue item, or a genuinely distinct thing). Sections 4 and 5 answer all four directly.

## 2. What the existing machinery already affords (the ground truth)

Before proposing anything, inventory what a decision already leaves behind today.

- The Open Questions queue records every decision permanently. A resolved item becomes `decided -> folded into <slug>`, the decision and its reasoning are folded into the affected step, and the item stays in the queue marked resolved so it is never re-addressed (`AGENTS.md:41`, `AGENTS.md:59`; plan Documentation Protocol at `docs/plans/agent-scaffold.md:36`). Crucially, the queue items in THIS plan already carry, in prose, exactly the contract's four parts. `Q-41` (`docs/plans/agent-scaffold.md:120`) records both explorer options (A guidance-only, B drops a working file), each option's rationale against numbered principles, the human's selection (Option A), and the reasoning. `Q-40` (`docs/plans/agent-scaffold.md:119`) records the chosen approach "over a new Roadmap override status/marker ... and over a new accepted-risk annotation field", i.e. the alternatives and why they lost. The queue item, written to the existing standard, is already a decision receipt in prose: options presented, recommendation, choice, principle-judged reasoning.
- The permanent metrics JSONL already carries two partial PROCESS receipts. `type:"escalation"` records a cap-escalation decision with `human_decision` (`decision` or `resume`) (`AGENTS.md:134`, `src/metrics.rs:311-316`); `type:"intake"` records a human interrupt with `classification` and `replanned` (`AGENTS.md:136`, `src/metrics.rs:323-328`). Both are permanent, committed, cross-task, and validated (`src/metrics.rs:422`). These attest that a specific class of human-decision event happened.
- AskUserQuestion (the harness surface used to put a decision to the human) presents the options and records the human's structured selection among them. Choosing Y from {X, Y, Z} inherently records that {X, Y, Z} was shown. This selection is captured in the harness transcript, outside the workflow's hand-authored artifacts.
- Git history commits all of the above: the queue prose, the JSONL records, and the commit that folds the decision into its step.

An honest note on the "partial receipts already exist" framing: the live log has exactly ONE `escalation` record (`optional-modules-inc2cii`, record present in `docs/metrics/workflow.jsonl`) and ZERO `intake` records. So in practice these process receipts are minted rarely, only at the two rare event types (a cap escalation, an interrupt), not at the common case (an ordinary open-question decision). The common decision path leaves its trace in the QUEUE, not the log.

## 3. The design space (null / light option is first-class)

Three viable options, plus the precise SSOT distinction that decides between them.

- Option A0 (NULL): build nothing new. Rely on the queue item (which already carries options + trade-offs + recommendation + choice + principle reasoning in prose), plus the `escalation`/`intake` records for the two event types, plus AskUserQuestion's recorded selection, plus git. Optionally tighten the queue-item guidance in prose so "record the options presented and the human's choice inline" is stated as the item standard, without any new check.
- Option A1 (LIGHT, derive don't duplicate): keep ONE home (the queue), and extend the EXISTING `validate --plan` (`src/plan.rs`) so a `decided -> folded into <slug>` item is held to a light STRUCTURAL completeness bar: it must point at a real Roadmap step (a cross-reference `validate --plan` largely does already) and carry a non-empty rationale body. No new JSONL record type, no new `validate --workflow` mode. This is `Q-42` candidate (b) trimmed to reuse machinery that already exists.
- Option A2 (HEAVY): mint a new `type:"decision"` JSONL receipt (question id, options presented, recommendation, human's choice) and a W4-analog check in `src/workflow.rs` that every `decided` queue item has a matching receipt. This is `Q-42` candidate (a).

The SSOT distinction that separates these, stated precisely. The queue holds the decision CONTENT: what was decided and why. A receipt is proposed to attest the PROCESS: that the contract-shaped presentation happened, recorded as the options presented plus the human's selection among them (choosing Y from {X, Y, Z} proves {X, Y, Z} was shown). The question Principle 16 forces: is that attestation a distinct thing, or does it collapse into the queue? The answer is a vise:

- The STRONG form of the receipt (options + recommendation + choice) is exactly the content a well-written `decided` queue item already carries (`Q-41` is the existence proof). Minting it as a separate JSONL record creates two hand-authored homes for one decision. If they disagree (the queue says "chose A over B and C"; the receipt lists options {A, B}), neither is derived from the other, so there is no authority to resolve the drift. That is a direct Principle 16 violation.
- The NON-DUPLICATIVE form (a bare "a contract-shaped presentation for Q-42 happened" flag pointing at the queue item, carrying no options of its own) does not collide with the queue, but it proves almost nothing: it is the self-certified "I presented the contract" flag the `Q-42` design nuance explicitly warns is weaker than recording the options + selection.

So you cannot get strong proof without duplicating the queue, and the non-duplicative version is the weak self-attestation the human wanted to avoid. The escalation/intake records escape this vise ONLY because they attest a different fact and carry data the queue does not (a `classification`, a `replanned` calibration boolean, an escalation event that a downstream check consumes); a `type:"decision"` receipt that copies options + choice crosses out of process-event data and into the queue's territory, where it collides.

## 4. Each option's trade-offs, judged against the numbered Project Principles

Project Principles are numbered in `docs/plans/agent-scaffold.md:18-25` (project set) with the shipped set in `AGENTS.md:106-127`; I cite by the number the human named (2 minimal-by-default, 16 one-source-of-truth) plus 3 (ground decisions in evidence).

Option A0 (NULL):

- Principle 2 (minimal): best. Zero ceremony added to any decision point. The core is untouched.
- Principle 16 (one source of truth): best. The queue stays the single home for decision content; the escalation/intake records stay the single home for their process-event data; no new home is minted to drift.
- Principle 3 (ground in evidence): supported. No incident of a fabricated or absent human decision is on record; the two incidents that motivated the analogous check (the `pause.md` step committed outside the review loop, `docs/plans/agent-scaffold.md:106`) were about SILENT skips of an unsupervised orchestrator action, which is a different failure mode (see the W3-analogy analysis below). Building nothing until such an incident appears is the evidence-grounded default.
- Cost: no deterministic proof that a `decided` item was genuinely presented-and-chosen rather than authored by the orchestrator alone. This is the one real gap; whether it matters is judged below.

Option A1 (LIGHT, derive don't duplicate):

- Principle 2: good. One home, one existing validator extended, no new record type or CLI mode. The added ceremony is a structural bar the queue items already meet.
- Principle 16: good. Keeps the queue as the sole authority and DERIVES the check from it, rather than minting a parallel copy. This is the SSOT-safe way to honour the "make it checkable" thesis.
- Principle 3: acceptable. It buys a real but modest guarantee (a `decided` item is well-formed and points at a real step) without over-building.
- Cost: the check is STRUCTURAL, not semantic. It can confirm a `decided` item names a real step and has a body; it cannot confirm the body genuinely enumerates the options that were shown, because "were the options really presented" is not decidable from prose. So A1 raises the floor a little (no empty or dangling `decided` items) but does not deliver machine-verified proof of presentation. Honesty about that ceiling matters: A1 is a tidiness check, not a proof of contract adherence.

Option A2 (HEAVY):

- Principle 2: worst. A new JSONL record type, a new schema in `pack/instrument.md` and `src/metrics.rs`, a new W4 mode in `src/workflow.rs`, and a receipt minted at EVERY human-decision point (dozens across a task), not just the two rare event types. This is exactly the "complicate the core / add machinery to every path" that Principle 2 exists to resist.
- Principle 16: worst. As section 3 shows, the strong receipt duplicates the queue's decision content across two hand-authored homes with no derivation between them, the canonical drift setup.
- Principle 3: weakest. It is speculative (no motivating incident) and, decisively, it does not actually deliver the proof it promises. The receipt would be authored by the SAME orchestrator that authors the queue item; an orchestrator that would fabricate a decision would fabricate its receipt just as easily. A receipt hand-written by the potentially-fabricating party is not independent evidence. The only unforgeable-by-the-orchestrator evidence of a genuine decision is the human's OWN input event, the AskUserQuestion selection the human actually clicked, or the human's own commit / message, both of which already exist in the transcript and git, outside the workflow's hand-authored artifacts. So A2 pays full ceremony for a proof that is no stronger than the queue prose it duplicates.

The W3 analogy, examined rather than assumed. W3 (`src/workflow.rs:135`) earns its place because convergence is performed by the orchestrator (an LLM) UNSUPERVISED, and a skipped review is SILENT, invisible, and consequential: unreviewed code reaches main and no human is in the loop to notice. The human-input contract is structurally different: the human is PRESENT at every human-decision point by definition. A decision point where the contract was not presented is one where the human was handed a bare choice with no options or reasoning, and the human would notice the absence in the moment, exactly as they noticed the GOOD presentation on `Q-40`. There is no true analog of the silent skip, because you cannot silently fail to present to the party you are presenting to. A receipt would prove to the human a thing the human already witnessed. The residual failure mode a receipt could address is not "silent skip" but "orchestrator fabricates a decision the human never made", and per the A2 analysis above, a self-authored receipt cannot guard that; only capturing the human's own input can, and that is already captured.

The escalation record is the precedent that shows WHEN a process receipt earns its place. The `type:"escalation"` `human_decision:"decision"` record exists and is about to be given real load: `escalation-exempt` (`Q-40`, `docs/plans/agent-scaffold.md:654-660`) makes W3 READ that record as the authority for a human-authorised below-streak convergence. The record earns its keep because a downstream DETERMINISTIC CHECK consumes it. That is the discriminating rule: mint a process receipt exactly where a mechanical consumer needs one, not universally. No mechanical consumer needs a receipt for an ordinary open-question decision, so none should be minted for one.

## 5. Recommendation

Recommend Option A0 (build nothing new), with the door open to the A1 half-step ONLY if the project decides its self-consistency thesis must be honoured uniformly. Concretely:

1. Do not add a `type:"decision"` JSONL receipt and do not add a W4 check (reject A2 outright).
2. Optionally, tighten the WORDING in the human-input-contract section of `AGENTS.md` and in the plan's item-format guidance so that "a `decided` item records the options presented and the human's choice inline" is stated as the item standard. This is documentation of the practice `Q-41` already follows, at zero new machinery and zero SSOT risk. It directly serves the human's stated want (a durable, readable record that the presentation happened) using the home that already holds it.
3. If, and only if, the project judges that every rule the workflow states about itself must be mechanically checkable (the strong form of the self-consistency thesis), then take A1: extend the EXISTING `validate --plan` so a `decided -> folded into <slug>` item must point at a real Roadmap step and carry a non-empty rationale body. Keep it structural, keep it in the queue's single home, add no JSONL type. Frame it honestly as a well-formedness check, not a proof of presentation.

Reasoning. The strongest genuine proof the human wants (that the options were shown and the human chose among them) is ALREADY captured twice over: as prose in the queue item, and as the human's own recorded selection in the AskUserQuestion transcript and git. A new hand-authored receipt adds neither content nor an independent trust anchor: it duplicates the queue (Principle 16) and is authored by the same party that would fabricate the thing it claims to prove (so it fails Principle 3's "ground decisions in evidence" both by lacking a motivating incident and by not actually delivering verification). The value the human named, that the `Q-40` decision was sound because of the presentation, was delivered by the CONTRACT (the rule) and recorded by the QUEUE (the content); neither needed a receipt. The audience analysis confirms the low value: for the solo dogfooding user the receipt proves something they personally witnessed; for a future contributor the queue prose plus git already serve; only the self-consistency thesis creates any pull, and A1 honours it minimally by deriving from the one home rather than duplicating into a second.

Coupling to `escalation-exempt` (`Q-40`). That step's build is held pending this decision because both would make W3 read human-decision records. This recommendation KEEPS them cleanly separable: `escalation-exempt` reads the `escalation` record for the ONE decision point that feeds a deterministic check (W3's below-streak exemption), which is exactly the "mint a receipt where a consumer needs it" rule. Recommending no general decision receipt means `escalation-exempt` proceeds as specified, reading the single existing escalation record, with no new record type to design around. The two are coherent: the escalation record stays a targeted process receipt for its one consumer, and the general case gets no universal receipt.

## 6. What NOT to build (the YAGNI boundary)

- Do NOT add a `type:"decision"` JSONL record type. It duplicates the decided queue item's content across a second hand-authored home (Principle 16 drift), adds schema to `src/metrics.rs` and `pack/instrument.md`, and its "proof" is only as trustworthy as the orchestrator that also writes the queue, so it raises the trust floor by nothing.
- Do NOT add a `validate --workflow` W4 check that pairs every `decided` item with a receipt. It presumes the receipt above, inherits its SSOT problem, and enforces a correspondence between two artifacts that a single well-formed queue item already collapses into one.
- Do NOT attempt a SEMANTIC check that a queue item's prose "really" enumerates the options presented. Whether options were shown is not decidable from free text; a check that claims to verify it would give false confidence. The most a check can honestly assert is structural well-formedness (A1), and even that is optional.
- Do NOT mint a receipt at every human-decision point. The evidence (one escalation record, zero intake records in the live log) shows process receipts are worth minting only at rare, consequential event types that a downstream check consumes; universal minting is ceremony without a consumer.
- Do NOT put any receipt in the ledger (already fixed, restated here for completeness): the ledger is per-task and deleted at close, while decisions are permanent.
